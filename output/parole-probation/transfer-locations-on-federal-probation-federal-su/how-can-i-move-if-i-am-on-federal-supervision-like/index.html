How can I move if I am on federal supervision (like federal probation, federal supervised release, or federal parole)?

If you are currently incarcerated and preparing for your release:

You may be able to request a transfer to another district. The request must be submitted to your Case Manager with the Bureau of Prisons since you will not yet have a Probation Officer.

If you are formerly incarcerated and already release and living in the community:

The process depends on whether you are asking to move to a new residence within the same district OR to a new residence in a different district. Because federal supervision is based on districts, it is not a question of whether you are asking to move across state lines but, instead, whether you are asking to move into a different district. The Interstate Compact on Adult Offender Supervision (ICAOS) does not apply to federal supervision. In a nutshell, it‚Äôs easier to move within the same district than to move to a new one. The steps below outline both possibilities:

POSSIBILITY #1: If you are moving to a new residence within your current district, it‚Äôs suggested that you follow these steps:

Improving your chances of having a TRANSFER request approved

Your request is much more likely to be approved if you have a good track record ‚Äì clean drug tests, always going to your meetings with your Probation Officer, staying out of trouble with law enforcement.

Notify your Probation Officer that you want to change your address, and submit that address and the contact information for anyone else living at that address. You must get permission from your Probation Officer to move within your current U.S. Probation District‚Äîeven if it‚Äôs across the street.Your Probation Officer will investigate the new address‚Äîso long as it is located in the same U.S. Probation District. As part of that investigation, your Probation Officer will:

    Make sure the new address actually exists;
    Make sure that other people living at the new address are willing and able to have you in their home;
    Run a background check on everyone living at the new address;
    PLEASE NOTE: Since it is a standard condition for all people on federal supervision to avoid associating with anyone else who has a felony conviction, your request to move/transfer to live with someone who has been convicted of a felony will likely be denied.
    Make sure that everyone at the new address knows about and agrees to the ‚ÄúSearch Condition‚Äù of your supervision.[913]
    Make sure there are no weapons at the new address.

POSSIBILITY #2: If you are moving to a new residence outside of your current U.S. Probation District, it‚Äôs suggested that you follow these steps:

Before you ask for a formal transfer to a new district‚Äîwhich can be a longer, more challenging process‚Äîask your probation officer for what is called ‚Äúcourtesy supervision‚Äù by another district.

This technically keeps your case in the original district, but allows you to live in and travel to the district of your choice. Your probation officer AND the probation officer of the other district have to agree. After doing this, it is much easier to transfer to the courtesy district than to just transfer from one district to another without ‚Äúcourtesy supervision‚Äù being set up first.

If ‚Äúcourtesy supervision‚Äù is denied or doesn‚Äôt work out, you can still request a formal transfer.

Tell your Probation Officer that you want to change your address, and submit that address and the contact information for anyone else living at that address. You must get permission from your Probation Officer to move to a new address in a different U.S. Probation District.

Your Probation Officer must submit a ‚ÄúTransfer Investigation‚Äù to the new district.

The Transfer Investigation generally takes 30 days or longer, since both your current district and the new district must investigate your new proposed address and approve the transfer. As part of the ‚ÄúTransfer Investigation,‚Äù a Probation Officer in the receiving District will:

    Make sure the new address actually exists;
    Make sure that other people living at the new address are willing and able to have you in their home;
    Run a background check on everyone living at the new address;
    PLEASE NOTE: Since it is a standard condition for all people on federal supervision to avoid associating with anyone else who has a felony conviction, your request to move/transfer to live with someone who has been convicted of a felony will likely be denied.
    Make sure that everyone at the new address knows about and agrees to the ‚ÄúSearch Condition‚Äù of your supervision.
    Make sure there are no weapons at the new address.

The receiving District must approve or deny the transfer after conducting the ‚ÄúTransfer Investigation.‚Äù

The receiving district can deny your request to transfer/move for any reason. The sending district where you are currently supervised must wait for a response before it can act to transfer your supervision.[914]

IMPORTANT: Some districts have hard and fast rules about certain offenses‚Äîfor example, that they won‚Äôt accept people with sex offenses, or the receiving district will require a more intense investigation of that person.
