What were the major changes to the way that parole revocation hearings work under realignment (as of july 1, 2013)?

CHANGE TO WHO HEARS THE CASE

The biggest change is that local county Superior Courts are now responsible for hearing most parole revocation cases.[544] Before July 1, 2013, the Board of Parole Hearings (BPH) was responsible for hearing all parole revocation cases, and now that responsibility has shifted to the courts for most cases.[545] County Superior Court judges will be the ones hearing these cases in state court.[546] One of the ideas behind this change was to create more transparency and fairness in parole revocation hearings‚Äîby having an independent, unbiased judge hear the case and weigh the evidence.

NOTE FOR ‚ÄúLIFERS‚Äù:

This change in the law means that if you were a ‚Äúlifer‚Äù in prison, your parole revocation hearing will be before the BPH, not the local county superior court.

However, the BPH will continue to hear certain cases. The BPH will continue to hear:

    Parole consideration for lifers (also called ‚Äúparole suitability hearings‚Äù);
    Medical parole hearings;
    Cases regarding mentally disordered offenders‚Äù (MDOs); and
    Cases regarding ‚Äúsexually violent predators.‚Äù

CHANGE TO WHERE YOU SERVE A SENTENCE FOR A PAROLE VIOLATION

    If your parole is revoked for a parole violation that occurred on or after July 1, 2013, you will be sentenced to COUNTY JAIL, not state prison.
    If your parole is revoked for a parole violation that occurred before July 1, 2013, you would have been sent back to state prison, so this is a big change in the law!
    THERE IS ONE EXCEPTION: If you are a parolee classified as a ‚Äúserious offender‚Äù (meaning you were a life-term prisoner under California Penal Code ¬ß¬†3000.1 or convicted of certain types of sex offenses under California Penal Code ¬ß¬†3000(a)(4), you will be sent back to CDCR to serve time in state prison for a parole revocation, and then you will go back to the BPH, who retains jurisdiction, for future consideration in determining whether or not to discharge you back onto parole.[547]

HOW LONG YOU SERVE YOUR SENTENCE

If you serve your parole violation (revocation) term in country jail, you will serve a MAXIMUM of 180 days.[548] Most people do serve their parole revocation terms in county jails.

But, if you are a former lifer, you may be sentenced to a maximum of 12 months in prison for a parole violation.[549] People who have maximum parole periods that are life-long (some people convicted to sex offenders or murder) may be sentenced to up to a year in prison for revocations, unless, at sometime during that year sentence, BPH determines that the person should be incarcerated longer.[550]
